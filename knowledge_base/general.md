# General Pet Care Guide

## Scheduling Best Practices
Pets thrive on routine. Keep feeding, walk, and medication times consistent day-to-day—variation causes stress and behavioral issues. When multiple pets share a household, stagger feeding times or use separate spaces to prevent food guarding. High-priority tasks (medications, insulin injections) should be scheduled at fixed times and never skipped. Use reminders or a scheduling app to track recurring care tasks.

## Common Medication Types and Schedules
- **Flea/tick topicals**: Apply once a month on the skin between the shoulder blades. Rotate application sites slightly each month. Most require 24-48 hours to fully dry; avoid bathing during this period.
- **Oral flea/tick tablets**: Given with food once monthly or every 3 months depending on the product.
- **Heartworm prevention**: Monthly oral tablet given on the same date each month. Never skip more than one dose without testing first.
- **Antibiotics**: Give the full course as prescribed—do not stop early even if symptoms resolve. Most are given every 12 or 24 hours with or without food per vet instructions.
- **Anti-inflammatory/pain medications (NSAIDs)**: Always give with food. Never give human ibuprofen or naproxen to pets—these are toxic to cats and dogs.
- **Insulin**: Typically given every 12 hours immediately after a meal; never inject without the pet having eaten.

## Dental Care
Dental disease affects 80% of pets over age 3 and is linked to heart, kidney, and liver problems. Brush teeth 2-3 times weekly using pet-safe toothpaste (human toothpaste contains xylitol, which is toxic to dogs). Dental chews and water additives provide supplemental support but do not replace brushing. Annual professional cleanings under anesthesia are recommended for most adult pets.

## Multi-Pet Household Tips
Introduce new pets gradually using scent swapping and visual barriers before direct contact. Feed pets in separate locations to prevent resource guarding. Provide one litter box per cat plus one extra (for cats). Ensure each pet has a private retreat space. Monitor for bullying, especially during feeding or at resting spots.

## Pet-Proofing Hazards
Common household toxins: chocolate, xylitol (in sugar-free gum/candy), grapes and raisins, onions and garlic, macadamia nuts, alcohol, caffeine, lilies (especially toxic to cats), sago palm, and many common houseplants. Secure trash cans, electrical cords, and cleaning supplies. Use pet-safe pest control products.

## When to Go to the Emergency Vet
Seek emergency care immediately for: difficulty breathing, suspected poisoning or ingestion of foreign object, uncontrolled bleeding, seizures, inability to stand or sudden paralysis, loss of consciousness, eye injury, heatstroke (excessive panting, drooling, bright red gums), suspected urinary blockage (straining without output), severe vomiting or diarrhea with blood.
