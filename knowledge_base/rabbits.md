# Rabbit Care Guide

## Feeding Schedule
Rabbits should have unlimited access to fresh timothy hay—this should make up 80% of their diet and is critical for digestive health. Leafy greens (romaine lettuce, kale, cilantro, parsley) should be offered twice daily, about 1 cup per 2 lbs body weight. Pellets should be limited: ¼ cup per 5 lbs body weight per day. Fresh water must always be available; a heavy ceramic bowl is better than a bottle. Avoid sugary treats, iceberg lettuce, and cabbage.

## Exercise and Free-Roam Time
Rabbits need a minimum of 3-4 hours of free-roam time outside their enclosure daily. A rabbit-proofed space is essential—they chew cables, baseboards, and furniture. Provide cardboard boxes, tunnels, and toys for enrichment. Rabbits are most active at dawn and dusk, so schedule exercise time accordingly. A pen of at least 4x4 feet is the minimum enclosure size for one rabbit.

## Medications and Preventatives
- Vaccines: RHDV2 (rabbit hemorrhagic disease virus) vaccine annually where available—critical in regions where the disease is present
- Parasite control: monthly Revolution (selamectin) if E. cuniculi or mites are a concern
- Spay/neuter: strongly recommended by 4-6 months; reduces cancer risk significantly in females
- Never give rabbits medications intended for other animals, especially antibiotics like amoxicillin, which can be fatal

## Vet Visits
- Initial: within 1 week of adoption
- Adults: annual wellness exam with a rabbit-savvy exotic vet
- Seniors (5+ years): biannual exams; dental checks more frequently as teeth problems are common

## Grooming
- Short-haired rabbits: brush once a week; daily during heavy molts (shedding seasons in spring/fall)
- Long-haired rabbits (Angora): brush daily to prevent wool block
- All rabbits: trim nails every 4-6 weeks; check teeth monthly for overgrowth; never bathe in water (can cause fatal hypothermia or shock)

## Warning Signs Requiring Urgent Vet Attention
GI stasis (not eating, no droppings, hunched posture) is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate vet care. Also watch for: head tilt, labored breathing, bleeding, paralysis, teeth grinding (pain), discharge from eyes or nose, soft cecotropes not being consumed.
