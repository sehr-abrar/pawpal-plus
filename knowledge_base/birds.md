# Bird Care Guide

## Feeding Schedule
The diet depends heavily on species. Parrots and cockatiels thrive on a pelleted diet (60-70%) supplemented with fresh vegetables and limited fruit. Budgies and finches do well on a quality seed mix supplemented with fresh greens daily. Offer fresh food in the morning and remove uneaten portions after 4 hours to prevent spoilage. Fresh water must be changed daily. Avoid avocado, chocolate, caffeine, onion, garlic, and high-salt or high-fat foods—these are toxic to birds.

## Exercise and Social Time
Birds are highly social and intelligent; lack of interaction causes feather-destructive behavior and depression. Provide a minimum of 2-3 hours of supervised out-of-cage time daily. Rotate toys weekly. Forage feeding (hiding food in toys) stimulates natural behavior. Parrots, cockatiels, and conures need daily social interaction with their owners or another bird. Budgies and finches do well in pairs or groups.

## Medications and Preventatives
- Vaccines: no standard vaccines for pet birds currently in widespread use
- Annual bloodwork and fecal gram stain recommended for early detection of infections
- Feather/beak/nail trims: by an avian vet or experienced groomer; never clip flight feathers too short
- Toxic household hazards: PTFE/Teflon non-stick cookware fumes are lethal to birds even in small amounts; also avoid aerosol sprays, scented candles, and cigarette smoke

## Vet Visits
- Initial: within 1 week of adoption (baseline bloodwork and cultures)
- Adults: annual exam with an avian vet
- Seniors: biannual exams; birds often hide illness until advanced stages, so regular checkups are critical

## Grooming
- Nail trims: every 6-8 weeks, by a vet or groomer; overgrown nails can get caught and cause injury
- Beak trims: only if overgrown; a healthy beak typically maintains itself with proper toys and perches
- Bathing: most birds enjoy misting 2-3 times per week; helps with feather condition and prevents itching

## Warning Signs Requiring Urgent Vet Attention
Fluffed feathers with eyes closed during the day, tail bobbing while breathing, discharge from nares or eyes, loss of balance, seizures, changes in droppings (color, consistency, volume), significant weight loss (weigh weekly on a gram scale), or sudden behavior change. Birds mask illness well—by the time symptoms are obvious, the situation is often critical.
