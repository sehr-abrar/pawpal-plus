# Cat Care Guide

## Feeding Schedule
Adult cats do well with 2 meals per day. Kittens under 6 months need 3-4 small meals daily. Cats are obligate carnivores and require high-protein diets. Wet food supports hydration and urinary tract health. Dry food can be offered as a supplement but should not be the sole diet. Free-feeding dry food often leads to obesity. Always provide fresh water; many cats prefer running water (consider a pet fountain).

## Exercise and Enrichment
Indoor cats need 10-15 minutes of active play at least twice daily to prevent obesity and boredom. Use feather wands, laser pointers, or puzzle feeders. Provide vertical space (cat trees, shelves) for climbing. Rotate toys to keep cats engaged. Interactive play is most effective in the morning and evening when cats are naturally more active.

## Medications and Preventatives
- Flea prevention: monthly topical treatment (e.g., Revolution, Advantage) even for indoor cats
- Heartworm prevention: monthly in high-risk areas (no treatment once infected, so prevention is critical)
- Core vaccines: Rabies annually or every 3 years; FVRCP (feline viral rhinotracheitis, calicivirus, panleukopenia) every 1-3 years after kitten series
- FeLV vaccine: recommended for outdoor or multi-cat household cats
- Deworming: every 3-6 months depending on indoor/outdoor status

## Vet Visits
- Kittens: every 3-4 weeks from 6-16 weeks for vaccines and spay/neuter consultation
- Adults (1-10 years): annual wellness exam
- Seniors (10+ years): biannual exams; bloodwork and urinalysis annually to monitor kidneys and thyroid

## Grooming
- Short-haired cats: brush once or twice a week to reduce shedding and hairballs
- Long-haired cats (Persian, Maine Coon): brush daily to prevent matting
- All cats: trim nails every 2-3 weeks; check ears monthly; dental treats or brushing weekly
- Never bathe unless medically necessary—cats self-groom effectively

## Warning Signs Requiring Urgent Vet Attention
Not eating for more than 24 hours (especially dangerous for cats—can cause hepatic lipidosis), straining to urinate or no urination (urinary blockage—emergency in male cats), open-mouth breathing, sudden hiding or behavior change, weight loss, excessive thirst, or vomiting more than twice a week.
